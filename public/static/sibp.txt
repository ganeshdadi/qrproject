Architectural Controls for OS-Level Autonomous Agents in the Banking Sector: A Synthesis of Blockchain Ledgers, Contextual Permissioning, and Multi-Factor AuthorityThe global financial infrastructure is approaching a technological event horizon where the primary interface for banking shifts from human-operated applications to autonomous agents residing within the operating system (OS). These OS-level agents—integrated into platforms such as Windows 11, iOS, and bespoke hardware—are transitioning from passive information retrievers into active economic participants capable of executing multi-step financial workflows. While this evolution promises unprecedented efficiency, it introduces a "lethal trifecta" of systemic risks: probabilistic prompt injection vulnerabilities, autonomous execution capacity, and privileged access to sensitive financial resources.Traditional cybersecurity frameworks, which rely on deterministic rule sets, are fundamentally ill-equipped to govern the stochastic reasoning of large language models (LLMs) that power these agents. This report details a novel, integrated control architecture known as the Sentinel Intent-Binding Plane (SIBP). By synthesizing blockchain-based activity ledgers, context-aware permissioning, and hardware-anchored multi-factor agent authentication, the SIBP provides a verifiable governance layer that ensures agent actions remain strictly bounded by human intent.The Sentinel Intent-Binding Plane (SIBP) ArchitectureThe SIBP acts as a non-bypassable "Control Plane" positioned between the OS-level agent and the bank's core APIs. As part of exploring emerging AI and edge-computing technologies within the engineering group, we are currently facing several practical limitations in our standard enterprise environment.

Today, our laptops and systems have restrictions such as:

- No Docker/container runtime access
- No admin privileges
- No ability to run local/open-source LLMs
- No support for edge AI experimentation
- Restrictions on installing low-level AI/ML tooling and dependencies

These controls are understandable and important for production and enterprise security. However, they also make it difficult for engineers to experiment with upcoming technologies, build prototypes, and evaluate innovation opportunities relevant to the banking domain.

To address this, we propose a small isolated innovation sandbox environment with a limited set of dedicated devices that can be used safely outside the standard production-controlled setup.

Below are the proposed devices and their purpose:

1. Mac Studio (M5 Ultra – 2026 Model)
   Purpose:
   High-performance AI workstation for running local LLMs, AI coding tools, multimodal AI models, vector databases, and advanced experimentation workloads.

Why needed:
Provides sufficient compute and memory to test modern AI use cases locally without depending entirely on cloud resources.

2. NVIDIA Jetson AGX Orin / Industrial
   Purpose:
   Edge AI experimentation device for testing lightweight/offline AI models, computer vision, voice AI, and embedded AI scenarios.

Why needed:
Helps us explore future banking use cases such as smart kiosks, branch automation, ATM intelligence, offline AI assistants, and on-device inference.

3. Apple iPad Pro (M4/M5) and/or iPhone 16 Pro+
   Purpose:
   Testing on-device AI capabilities and mobile AI experiences.

Why needed:
Allows experimentation with AI-powered mobile banking features, voice interactions, accessibility use cases, OCR, offline AI workflows, and Apple on-device AI frameworks.

4. Mac Mini M4 Pro (64GB RAM)
   Purpose:
   Compact shared AI server/lab machine for Docker, Kubernetes, local model hosting, vector databases, and lightweight AI infrastructure testing.

Why needed:
Cost-effective system for creating a small internal AI experimentation environment without requiring large enterprise infrastructure changes.

Expected Benefits:

- Faster innovation and prototyping
- Ability to evaluate upcoming AI technologies safely
- Exploration of edge AI and on-device AI use cases
- Better understanding of future banking AI opportunities
- Reduced dependency on external/cloud experimentation
- Improved engineering capability in modern AI tooling and infrastructure

Security Consideration:
The proposal is for a separate isolated sandbox/non-production environment with no customer or production data access. The environment can remain fully segregated from enterprise production systems while still enabling controlled innovation and experimentation.


Slide 2 — Why Traditional Security Is No Longer Enough

Traditional banking security was designed for:

human users,

deterministic applications,

fixed workflows,

predictable system behavior.


AI agents behave fundamentally differently:

they reason probabilistically,

dynamically decide next steps,

chain multiple tools together,

adapt workflows in real time.


Current security systems mainly answer:

“Is this the correct user?”

“Is the password/session valid?”


They do NOT answer:

“Is the AI still pursuing the original approved goal?”

“Has the AI been manipulated mid-workflow?”

“Should this action still be allowed in this context?”


AI agents can inherit the same permissions as the user:

banking access,

logged-in sessions,

financial APIs,

corporate systems,

sensitive documents.


This creates a new risk model where:

authenticated actions can still be malicious,

valid sessions can still execute fraud,

compromised reasoning can bypass traditional controls.


Example risks:

hidden prompt injection in PDFs/emails/websites,

unauthorized beneficiary changes,

bulk customer data extraction,

manipulated investment decisions,

autonomous high-value transfers.


Traditional fraud detection often works after execution.

AI agents can execute thousands of actions before humans notice anomalies.


The industry needs a shift from:

identity-centric security

to intent-centric security.

Slide 3 — Introducing the Sentinel Intent-Binding Plane (SIBP)

The Sentinel Intent-Binding Plane (SIBP) is a governance and security layer designed for autonomous AI agents interacting with banking systems.

SIBP sits between:

AI agents,

MCP banking tools/APIs,

core financial systems.


The goal of SIBP is to ensure:

AI actions remain aligned with approved human intent,

autonomous actions stay within policy boundaries,

high-risk actions are contextually validated before execution.


SIBP introduces five core architectural controls:


1. Intent Binding

Converts human goals into machine-verifiable intent mandates.

Ensures AI actions remain tied to original approved objectives.


2. Semantic Decision Tokens (SDT)

Short-lived, single-use authorization tokens.

Only issued if the requested action matches approved intent and policy.


3. Contextual Permissioning

Permissions dynamically change based on:

location,

device trust,

risk level,

transaction amount,

time of access.



4. Hardware-Anchored Approval

High-risk actions require:

biometric approval,

hardware security key confirmation,

physical human verification.



5. Immutable Decision Provenance

Agent reasoning and authorization events are recorded in a tamper-resistant distributed ledger.

Provides regulator-grade auditability and forensic traceability.

SIBP changes the trust model from:

“Trust the authenticated session”

to

“Trust only verified intent-bound execution.”

Slide 4 — How SIBP Protects Autonomous Banking Workflows

Every autonomous banking action follows a controlled verification flow before execution.


Step 1 — Human or Policy Defines Intent

Examples:

“Pay approved invoices”

“Auto-pay utility bills below ₹5,000”

“Maintain low-risk investment strategy”

SIBP converts these into structured intent mandates.



---

Step 2 — Agent Initiates Banking Action

The AI agent:

reads emails/documents,

reasons about next steps,

invokes MCP banking tools.


Example:

mcp.bank.transfer_funds()


---

Step 3 — SIBP Validates Intent Consistency

SIBP checks:

Does the requested action match the original goal?

Is the recipient expected?

Is the amount within approved limits?

Is the behavior contextually safe?



---

Step 4 — Context & Risk Evaluation

Additional checks include:

geolocation,

device trust,

time of day,

unusual behavior,

transaction risk level,

policy violations.



---

Step 5 — Decision Provenance Logged

Reasoning traces and authorization decisions are stored in an immutable distributed ledger:

original intent,

requested action,

validation outcome,

approval chain,

policy evaluation.



---

Step 6 — Semantic Decision Token (SDT) Issued

Only if all checks pass:

SIBP issues a short-lived authorization token.

MCP banking tools execute only with valid SDTs.



---

Step 7 — High-Risk Actions Trigger Human Step-Up

Examples:

beneficiary changes,

high-value transfers,

bulk data access.


These require:

hardware MFA,

biometric confirmation,

explicit human approval.



---

SIBP ensures that:

autonomous agents remain governed,

financial actions remain bounded,

compromised reasoning cannot directly execute banking operations.
